
"Our existing taxes and charges are simply not adequate to provide the responsible and reasonable services in health and education the community expects us to provide," Mr Brumby said.

Mr Brumby argued that last week's federal budget cuts to the states had made the situation worse.

"We have a federation that over time has become increasingly out of balance," he said.

Mr Brumby told an audience at Melbourne University that the decision to shift $80 billion of spending from the states had made their "medium and long term budget position completely untenable and unsustainable".

Mr Brumby led Victoria from 2007 to 2010 and chairs the soon-to-be-defunct COAG Reform Council.

"If I was a state premier, I think I'd be putting up my hand to say, I'll have the GST thank you very much," he said.

He said revenues had fallen well short of the "rivers of gold" that were promised to states in the 1990s, when state taxes were replaced by the GST.

"Some of the states would probably be in a better fiscal position today if they just retained the state taxes they gave up as part of GST," he said.
