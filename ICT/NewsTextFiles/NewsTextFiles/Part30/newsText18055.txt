
Participants in a march from the Nigerian High Commission to Downing Street in London on Saturday call for the return of the schoolgirls who were kidnapped in Nigeria.

If there is any good to be salvaged out of a terrorist group’s kidnapping of schoolgirls and threats to sell them into slavery or marriage, it is the world’s unity against the act.

Seldom has an atrocity attracted such widespread disgust. Adjectives such as “vile”, “senseless” and “horrific” have echoed from one continent to another. World leaders, personalities and citizens have expressed abhorrence at armed men snatching 276 schoolgirls in an April 15 pre-dawn raid on Chibok Government Girls’ Secondary School in the north-east Nigerian province of Borno.

Boko Haram, a supposed Islamist group, wants to exchange the teens, and eight others they abducted elsewhere later, for prisoners belonging to the well-armed and increasingly diabolical movement. It has been behind a lot of bloodshed in the past four years or so.

“This group is armed, with heavy weapons of an unimaginable sophistication and the ability to use them,” said French President Francois Hollande on Saturday, after a crisis meeting in Paris involving the countries in that part of Africa plagued by Boko Haram.

The kidnappings more than a month ago, and the continuing holding of the girls, has sharpened the world’s attention on the group. But the act has done anything but popularise its cause – this is not the type of attention these fiends want. Offers of help have poured in from governments with formidable intelligence and military resources.

Nigerian President Goodluck Jonathan has been criticised for not getting to grips with this group. Global interest should spur his efforts. Saturday’s summit pledged to shore up border vigilance, pool intelligence and trace Boko Haram’s lifelines: cash and guns.

The shared outrage, from Desmond Tutu and the ANC Women’s League to US First Lady Michelle Obama and movie stars, shows a world trying to find and revive its moral core, re-establish a semblance of decency amid frequent atrocities and depravity.

In spite of Boko Haram’s claims, rhetoric and props, no devout group could offer a spiritual justification for such deeds.
